Job Quest: Turning resume writing and interview prep into a guided learning experience for entry-level job seekers

Apprenticeship Project at Co.Lab
Product Sector: Job Search
Team: 1 Product Manager, 1 Designer, 3 Developers
Duration: 8 weeks
Deliverables: Live Interactive Product, Documentation, Presentation
OVERVIEW

What should we build to help entry-level job seekers succeed?

Quest began as an exploration into reducing overwhelm in the job search process. Early on, we explored ways to support job seekers by gamifying the process through "Quests" and "Missions." However, through deeper investigation into the root cause of the problem and personal reflection, the focus of the project shifted.

As an entry-level job seeker myself, I experienced a recurring challenge: not knowing how to translate experience into clear, well-structured resume bullets that highlight impact, how to clearly talk about my projects, or how to avoid filler words during interviews.

The problem wasn't a lack of tools. It was a lack of guidance.

PROBLEM

Entry-level job seekers lack guidance, not tools.

Entry-level job seekers are often expected to present themselves confidently in resumes and interviews without learning how to do so. Many struggle to explain their work clearly, choose effective action verbs, or articulate impact, leading to overwhelm during interviews.

"42% of participants reported struggling to secure interviews despite actively job searching for 3 to 12 months."
— Interview Participant

In our interviews and survey responses, this gap isn't about effort. It's about understanding and guidance. Without guidance, job seekers resort to over-reliance on templates, overusing filler words, or relying on tools that generate content without teaching transferable skills.

RESEARCH

Researching the job search landscape and current tools

We went deep into understanding the job search ecosystem, existing tools, and the challenges entry-level job seekers face. We researched existing platforms and analyzed their approaches to resume building and interview preparation.

OPPORTUNITY

Guidance as the core of job search learning.

The biggest barrier for entry-level job seekers isn't access to tools. There is, in fact, a surplus of them. Instead, users are unfamiliar with the structure needed to explain their own experience.

This insight reframed the product direction. Instead of optimizing for output, Quest evolved into a guidance-first learning experience, similar to Duolingo, designed to help entry-level job seekers learn how to:

  • Write meaningful resume bullets that highlight impact
  • Speak confidently about their projects and experience
  • Practice interview responses without relying on scripted answers

With a guidance-first approach, Job Quest and its users will benefit from:

Teaching Transferable Skills
Users learn why certain techniques work, not just what to use.
Building Confidence
Guided practice helps users articulate their experience more clearly.
Long-term Growth
Focus on skill-building over quick fixes creates lasting impact.
CONSTRAINTS & PRIORITIZATION

Exploring product direction and prioritization

With an 8-week timeline and limited development capacity, we prioritized features that supported learning and guidance rather than automation. We used the ICE prioritization framework (Impact, Confidence, Effort).

Strategic directions we explored:

Automated Tools
Fully automated resume rewriting and content generation.
Matching Features
Jobs or roles matching features to connect users with opportunities.
Learning Platforms
Learning, practicing, and studying tools modeled after platforms like Quizlet.

While these features could improve efficiency and motivation, they risked prioritizing speed over learning and bypassing the guided practice and repetition needed for users to truly understand why certain job search strategies work.

SOLUTION

Job Quest: the Guided Learning Experience that teaches job search skills, not replaces them.

To support learning without creating dependency on tools that automate content, we designed assistive patterns that focus on how to communicate ideas, because telling users what to say does not build long-term confidence.

Let us explore our attempt to solving the problem.

DESIGN DECISIONS

We identified the most intuitive way to bring guidance to life without relying on automation.

Insight 1: Guidance with explanations, not just outputs

Suggestions are paired with explanations that help users understand why certain wording works, not just what to use.

Insight 2: "Help me understand" option

A "Help me understand" option allows users to explore the reasoning behind changes, building deeper understanding.

REFLECTION

What I learned

Guidance matters more than automation.

Early-career users benefit most from support that helps them understand why things work, not just tools that speed up output.

Confidence comes from practice.

Repeated, guided practice helps users articulate their experience more clearly and reduces anxiety in resumes and interviews.

Designing for learning changes success metrics.

When the goal is skill-building, success is measured by understanding and confidence, not just completion.

What I'm Proud Of

Strong direction under constraints.

Within a short timeline, we aligned on a clear, learning-first vision and made intentional tradeoffs to support users' growth.

Next Steps

Validate learning outcomes.

Test whether guided practice improves users' ability to explain their work over time.

Expand guided lessons.

Extend the learning model to networking and behavioral interview scenarios.
